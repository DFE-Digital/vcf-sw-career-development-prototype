Key takeaways

High quality supervision can support wellbeing and contribute to improved retention.

  • practitioners' wellbeing can be enhanced, and the risk of stress and burnout reduced, through the provision of good-quality reflective supervision.
  • outcomes for children and young people can be improved through direct practice which is supported through effective supervision.

Reflective learning and organisational cultures should be prioritised.

  • organisational cultures prioritising learning are more likely to support and develop high-quality supervision.
  • practitioners benefit from working in organisations that value and reinforce reflection and learning, improving recruitment, retention and reputation
  • leaders who recognise that learning can be accelerated through developing a ‘no blame’ culture and actively encouraging curiosity about why things happen are more likely to create the conditions for high-quality reflective learning.

Support is crucial.

  • at all levels, effective leaders offer support and encourage open, reflective communication, feedback, and discussion.
  • being a role model for emotionally literate leadership and mindful listening can encourage people to seek out reflective space and guidance when making complex decisions.
  • support needs to be delivered within a culture which is psychologically safe.
  • having role models for culturally sensitive leadership is particularly important if people from the global majority are to feel held and supported.

Supervisor learning and development is important.

  • a well-developed, responsive supervisor, capable of managing the emotional complexity of social work practice, is more likely to deliver high-quality reflective supervision.
  • evidence suggests that systemically informed supervision is likely to be more

Both quality and quantity of supervision must be measured.

  • frequent poor-quality social work supervision or infrequent high-quality reflective opportunities is not enough to support effective practice consistently.
  • audits of social work practice should not only focus on measuring the frequency of supervision, the quality of supervisory recordings, or practitioners' self-reported.
