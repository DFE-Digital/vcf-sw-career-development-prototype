Supervision Standard

Throughout all stages of their professional development, from student to experienced practitioner, individuals should have opportunities for high-quality supervision. This allows them to critically reflect on their practice, strategically plan purposeful and meaningful interventions with people using services and voice any concerns they may have. Critical reflection should be challenging, fostering professional curiosity and learning, ensuring that supervision serves as a practice-shaping space to offer the best possible support to service users1. Employers are expected to ensure this type of supervision is provided.

The annual health check will ask social workers and other practitioners in your organisation to express their level of agreement with several statements concerning their experiences of supervision. The areas covered include:

  • do they have the opportunity for frequent, scheduled, and uninterrupted supervision with a skilled supervisor who is a registered social worker? If not, do they feel able to raise concerns about this with you or another appropriate person in your organisation?
  • to what extent does the supervision that practitioners receive: Help them develop their professional judgment and autonomous decision making? Support them to shape their practice with service users? Support them to critically reflect on the emotional and relational aspects of their work? Provide opportunities for the application of evidenced-informed and theoretical approaches to practice?
  • whether they are given sufficient opportunities to consider their professional development, training, and learning requirements, and whether they can reflect on how they are meeting the requirements of their professional body.

During inspections, inquiry will centre on whether supervision is critically reflective, safe, and emotionally supportive, yet challenging. The extent to which supervision aids the personal and professional development of workers is also assessed. Additionally, Ofsted mandates that supervision should be appropriately recorded.

As part of their professional standards, Social Work England (2022) 3 requires reflective supervision not only to serve as a mechanism for evaluating practice, but also to provide an opportunity for workers to discuss the emotional impact of the work on their own wellbeing.

Additional requirements relating to practice supervisors and educators are as follows:

  • if you are a senior leader or principal social worker in adult care, you must ensure that supervisors comply with the post-qualifying Standards for Social Work Practice Supervisors in adult social care4. These standards outline in detail what practice supervisors are expected to do, the values they must uphold, and how their supervisory practice should be carried out.
  • if you are a senior leader or principal social worker in children and families social work, you must ensure supervisors comply with the Post-qualifying Standard, Knowledge and Skills Statement for Child and Family Practice Supervisors5.
  • all employers who provide placements for social work students must also ensure that the practice educators who supervise them adhere to the Practice Educator Professional Standards (PEPS6).

Some useful evidence-informed resources and tools are available to support effective supervision in social care. The Social Care Institute for Excellence (SCIE) has developed a guide to effective supervision7. Research in Practice provides tools to support practice supervisors in both children and family social work8 and adult social care9.
