What does the Supervision standard mean for Leaders?

As the strategic lead for social work in your organisation, it is your responsibility to ensure that social workers and student social workers have frequent opportunities for high-quality supervision.

The standard clearly outlines the requirements for both the frequency and duration of supervision for both students and experienced practitioners. Supervision must be an uninterrupted session lasting at least 90 minutes. For experienced workers, it should occur monthly, while those in their first year of practice should receive supervision fortnightly. Additionally, students should receive supervision as outlined in their course requirements.

To support these requirements, a supervision policy must be in place at a strategic level. You, as leader, are required to monitor the frequency and quality of supervision within your organisation. Importantly, monitoring and auditing of this standard should not be treated as an isolated activity but incorporated into your organisation’s broader accountability framework. This integration ensures a comprehensive and continuous commitment to the standards outlined for supervision.

The standard defines the expectations for the quality of supervision, mandating that it must:

  • enable critical reflection on practice, identifying the learning needs
  • promote continuous learning and knowledge sharing
  • evaluate capability and developmental needs

As an employer, you are required to establish systems ensuring that supervision:

  • supports qualified practitioners and students in meeting professional regulatory standards
  • provides additional supervision if practitioners are not managed by a registered social worker
  • ensures regular training in place for social workers who act as supervisors

A widely agreed definition of supervision describes it as “a process by which one worker is given responsibility by the organisation to work with another in order to meet certain organisational professional and personal objectives which together promote the best outcomes for service users” (Morrison, 2005).

The standard implies that social work supervision has four main functions:

  • support: ensuring social workers and students feel supported in their roles.
  • management: ensuring competent and accountable practice.
  • mediation: facilitating engagement between the individual with the organisation.
  • development: promoting and engaging in continuing professional development.

Although identified separately, the four functions are interconnected and need to be well balanced. As outlined by Carpenter et al. (2013: 1844) “the ultimate goal of professional supervision should be to provide the best possible support to service users in accordance with the organization's responsibilities and accountable professional standards.”
