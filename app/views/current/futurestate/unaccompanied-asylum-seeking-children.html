Unaccompanied asylum-seeking children

Summary
Support the safety, well-being and developmental needs of unaccompanied asylum-seeking children

What you'll do

Your responsibilities will vary depending on your role and position in the team.

Social workers in unaccompanied asylum-seeking children teams are expected to:

  • safeguard and protect the rights and welfare of asylum-seeking children
  • assess any risks or vulnerabilities children may face to ensure they are safe and supported
  • help children access essential services such as healthcare, education and accommodation
  • provide trauma-informed support to help children cope with past experiences and build resilience for the future
  • ensure children are reunited with their parents or legal guardians
  • help children access education and integration programs in their new community
  • advocate for the rights of children including access to essential services, fair treatment and legal protection

Skills and knowledge

The skills and knowledge you need will vary depending on your role and position in the team.

Social workers in unaccompanied asylum-seeking children teams are expected to have:

  • interpersonal skills to communicate with children clearly and compassionately
  • knowledge of immigration and asylum systems, legal frameworks and procedures
  • knowledge of trauma-informed practice to address the impact of trauma on children
  • the ability to assess and respond to risks or concerns related to the safety and well-being of children
  • cross-cultural communication skills to engage with individuals who may have limited English ability or use interpreters
  • knowledge of accessing essential services such as healthcare, education, housing and legal representation
  • familiarity with the welfare system to help children access social support and entitlements
  • awareness of the legal and ethical issues related to working with vulnerable populations

It could also be useful to have:

  • an advanced qualification in asylum processes, trauma-informed practice or cross-cultural communication
  • knowledge of languages commonly spoken by asylum-seekers and refugees such as Arabic, Farsi, Dari or Tigrinya

Who you'll work with

You could work with external partners and services including:

  • schools
  • health services
  • housing departments
  • mental health services
  • community support organisations
  • immigration agencies
  • legal representatives
  • charities and non-governmental agencies

How you'll work

Working conditions can vary, but you could:

  • be office-based on work remotely, or a combination of both
  • work full-time or part-time
  • work flexible hours, compressed hours or have a job share
  • travel?
  • support systems in place for the team, such as supervision, training, and access to resources?
