Workforce and practice resources

Employers of social workers and other key professionals must meet eight Employers Standards for Social Workers in England. These standards are integral to a ’whole systems approach’ that supports employees in their practice. Aligning organisational policies and practice to the Employer Standards, is likely to support practitioner wellbeing and create the conditions for effective practice to thrive.

The Employer Standards (ES) are assessed through an annual Health Check; administered in local authorities and other social work-related employer organisations. The Health Check surveys social workers, approved mental health professionals, occupational therapists, and non-registered social care practitioners to gather information on their working conditions and environment. The findings offer insights into challenges within the domains outlined by the Employer Standards and identify areas for improvement.

These resources are designed to support leaders in addressing your organisation’s health check scores against each standard. Their purpose is to help you implement strategies that improve working conditions and optimise organisational culture. By taking these actions, you can enhance organisational functioning, promote workforce wellbeing, potentially boost recruitment and retention, and likely have a positive impact on direct practice. Additionally, the resources aim to guide organisational-level improvements through future health checks and offer evidence of action planning.

For Practitioners and Managers, the Employer Standards tell you what to expect from your employer and how each of the Employer Standards is designed to support your wellbeing, resilience and promote practice excellence. You will find useful tools and resources in each Employer Standard which focus on practice, management and leadership.

In each Employer Standard, Part 1 describes the key takeaways and provides a short summary of the ES.

In Part 2, you will find a range of targeted, evidence-informed tools and resources that will support your organisation to respond to areas flagged for action in the Health Check, and resources to support you to continue to improve and sustain high performance in each of the ES domains.

The resources are designed to support each standard, but a multi-level, systemic approach to implementing interventions is needed to sustain high-quality social work and social care practice while also promoting workforce wellbeing. Strategies are required at both the team and the organisational levels, and strategic awareness is necessary regarding the need for targeted, evidence-informed interventions at the individual level. Resources are likely to be relevant to more than one standard. For instance, supporting practitioner wellbeing (ES4) should not be seen in isolation; it relies on an environment where other standards such as Supervision (ES5) are also prioritised. To facilitate the utilisation of the resources across standards, a matrix has been developed to identify interventions relevant to improving more than one standard (see XXX).

As employers, you must establish a monitoring system to assess your performance against the Employer Standards framework, establish a review process, and delineate action plans for improvement. This set of resources is designed to help you to create these action plans for change, keeping good practice in mind.

A rigorous programme of research with social care workers in different contexts1,2, identified some key principles or ’golden threads‘ that underpin the conditions necessary for effective practice and wellbeing. These principles will be referred to in each of the resources. These key principles are:

  • valuing diversity and promoting equity and inclusion
  • recognising the value of co-production as a critical ingredient for change
  • demonstrating a strong commitment to maintaining values and building trust
  • establishing effective communication structures
  • fostering compassionate, emotionally literate leadership

When developing and implementing interventions, it is essential to consider the diverse needs of workers. Different strategies may be needed (for example, where employees are neurodiverse) requiring the use of a range of modalities for maximum benefit.
